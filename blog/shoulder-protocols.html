Shoulder Protocols for Striking Athletes: Building Bulletproof Overhead Function

Dylan Medina | Medina Fight Lab

Striking sports place extraordinary demand on the shoulder complex. Every punch thrown — cross, hook, uppercut — involves rapid eccentric loading, rotator cuff co-contraction, and scapular stabilization under fatigue. Over a fight camp, this accumulates fast. Most fighters don't train their shoulders in a way that matches these demands. Here's how to fix that.

Understanding the Demand

The shoulder in striking isn't a simple pushing mechanism. It's a dynamic stabilizer performing rapid load-transfer between the lower body and the fist. The rotator cuff — supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, and subscapularis — must fire eccentrically at the end of every punch to decelerate the arm and protect the joint. Neglect this and you're building toward impingement, labral stress, or AC joint issues.

The Protocol Framework

Shoulder training for strikers should follow a three-category approach:

1. Posterior Cuff & Deceleration Capacity

  • Band Pull-Aparts: 3 × 20 — primary scapular retraction and posterior cuff activation
  • Prone Y/T/W raises: 3 × 12 — mid and lower trap, rhomboids, infraspinatus
  • Eccentric External Rotation (side-lying): 3 × 15 slow — deceleration-specific loading
  • Cable Face Pulls: 3 × 15 — posterior deltoid, external rotators

2. Scapular Control Under Load

  • Serratus Wall Slides: 3 × 10 — serratus anterior activation, essential for upward rotation
  • Push-up Plus: 3 × 12 — integrates serratus into pressing patterns
  • Landmine Press: 3 × 8 per side — scapular rhythm in a sport-specific angle

3. Overhead Mobility & Joint Preparation

  • Thoracic extension over foam roller: 2 min daily
  • Open book rotations: 2 × 10 per side
  • 90/90 shoulder stretching: 2 × 45 sec per position

Programming Notes

Run this as a standalone shoulder prep block 2–3x per week in the off-season. During fight camp, reduce volume by 40% and prioritize the posterior cuff and scapular work. The overhead mobility work should be daily, even on rest days. If pain is present, see a physiotherapist — don't train through shoulder impingement.

The Bottom Line

Strikers need eccentric deceleration strength, scapular control, and thoracic mobility — not just more pressing. Build the posterior cuff, earn your overhead range, and your shoulder will hold up through fight camp and beyond.
