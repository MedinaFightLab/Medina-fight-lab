The 3 Energy Systems: How to Train Each One for Combat Sports

Dylan Medina | Medina Fight Lab

Every punch, clinch exchange, takedown, and scramble is powered by one or more of three energy systems. Understanding how they work — and how to train each — is the foundation of fight-specific conditioning.

System 1: The Phosphocreatine System (ATP-PCr)

Duration: 0–10 seconds | Intensity: Maximal

This is your explosive power system. It fuels maximal efforts — a knockout punch combination, an explosive double-leg, an all-out 3-second clinch war. It regenerates rapidly (50% in ~30 seconds, full recovery in ~3 minutes) but depletes just as fast.

How to Train It: Maximal effort intervals of 5–10 seconds with long rest (1:6–1:10 work:rest ratio). Think: 5-second all-out sprints, explosive med ball throws, maximal pad combinations. The goal is true maximal output every rep — if intensity drops, the rest period is too short.

System 2: The Glycolytic System (Anaerobic)

Duration: 10 seconds – 2 minutes | Intensity: High

This is the system most relevant to round-by-round combat. A full exchange on the feet, a sustained grappling scramble, a 45-second pressure-heavy combination — these are glycolytic events. This system produces ATP rapidly but generates lactate accumulation, which causes the burning sensation and force output drop you feel in late rounds.

How to Train It: Work intervals of 20–90 seconds at high intensity with moderate rest (1:2–1:3 work:rest). Bike sprints, heavy bag rounds with structured work periods, circuit training. Conditioning should feel hard but controlled — not sloppy or broken down.

System 3: The Aerobic System (Oxidative)

Duration: 2+ minutes | Intensity: Low to Moderate

The aerobic system is your recovery engine. Between explosive bursts, during clinch control, and round-to-round — your aerobic capacity determines how quickly you restore PCr, clear lactate, and return to baseline. A well-developed aerobic base means you stay sharper, longer.

How to Train It: Zone 2 cardio (nasal breathing, conversational pace) — running, cycling, row — 3–5 sessions/week of 30–60 minutes in the off-season. Cardiac output work: 30–60 min at 130–150 bpm. Tempo runs. This work is not glamorous, but it is foundational. Fighters who skip aerobic base work gas out in rounds 3–5 regardless of how hard they train technically.

Putting It Together

A complete fight conditioning program cycles emphasis across all three systems depending on training phase. Off-camp: heavy aerobic base + PCr development. Fight camp: glycolytic intervals dominate + aerobic maintenance. Peak week: activation only, full recovery.

Train all three. Neglect none.
