Weight Cutting for Combat Athletes: How to Make Weight Without Losing Performance

Dylan Medina | Medina Fight Lab

Making weight is one of the most poorly managed parts of a fighter's camp. Fighters who cut 10–15% of their body weight through acute dehydration in the final 24–48 hours are not competing in an advantage — they're competing in a deficit. The research is unambiguous: aggressive, rapid weight cuts impair power output, reaction time, and cognitive function, and the rehydration window most fighters get is not long enough to reverse the damage. The goal of a smart weight management strategy is to arrive at the scale lean, arrive at fight time recovered, and keep the actual water cut as small as possible.

Two Phases of Weight Management

Separating "making weight" into two distinct phases makes the problem easier to solve: the long cut and the acute cut. Most fighters conflate them and end up doing too much of both badly.

Phase 1: The Long Cut (Weeks to Months Out)

This is where body composition work happens. Reducing body fat through a modest caloric deficit during the off-season or early camp means less work to do in the final days. A rate of 0.5–1% of bodyweight per week is sustainable and preserves lean mass. Going faster than that risks muscle loss, which directly costs you strength and power — the opposite of what you want going into camp.

  • Target fight weight + 3–5% as your walk-around weight by the final week of camp. If you're more than 5% above your class limit at fight week, your cut is too aggressive and the fix is in the off-season, not the sauna.
  • Don't diet hard during fight camp. Heavy training demands fuel. Cutting calories aggressively while simultaneously ramping training load is a fast track to poor performance and injury. Save the lean-out phase for off-season.
  • Reduce dietary carbohydrates in the final 5–7 days to deplete glycogen stores, which hold roughly 3–4 g of water per gram of glycogen. This alone can drop 1–3 kg without touching sweat-based dehydration.

Phase 2: The Acute Cut (Final 24–48 Hours)

This is the water cut — and it should be as small as possible. Every percentage point of bodyweight lost through dehydration comes with a cost: strength drops at approximately 2% dehydration, reaction time and decision-making worsen, and cardiovascular capacity falls. A 5% acute cut with a poor rehydration window is a performance catastrophe dressed as a competitive strategy.

  • Limit the water cut to 3–4% of bodyweight whenever the rehydration window allows it. Anything above that requires increasingly longer recovery time that most fight formats don't give you.
  • Sweat-based methods (sauna, hot bath, exercise in layers) are more effective than diuretics or laxatives, which can cause dangerous electrolyte shifts. Stick to heat-based protocols.
  • Monitor urine colour throughout the process — you want pale yellow as your baseline and dark amber as your stop signal. Black or brown urine is a medical emergency.

The Rehydration Window — Where Most Fighters Lose the Fight

The cut only costs you if you don't recover. Full rehydration from a 5% water-weight deficit takes 24–36 hours under optimal conditions. Most combat sports offer 12–16 hours between weigh-ins and competition. That gap matters enormously.

What to Consume

Rehydration is not just water. Drinking plain water after a significant cut dilutes plasma sodium, blunts thirst, and actually slows the process. You need sodium to retain fluid and drive absorption.

  • Oral rehydration solution (ORS) or a high-sodium sports drink is the evidence-based approach. Aim for 500–1000 mg of sodium per litre of fluid.
  • Carbohydrates alongside sodium speeds gut absorption and begins glycogen replenishment simultaneously. A mix of glucose and fructose (as in many sports drinks) is absorbed faster than either alone.
  • Protein — aim for 30–40 g in the post-weigh-in meal to support muscle repair and satiety without overloading digestion before competition.
  • Avoid excess fibre and fat in the immediate rehydration window — both slow gastric emptying and can leave you feeling heavy or gassy during warm-up.

Rate of Rehydration

Don't pound fluids immediately after weigh-ins. The gut can only absorb roughly 800–1000 mL per hour. Drinking more than that just produces urine and discomfort. Spread your intake steadily across the available window rather than trying to force it all in the first hour.

  • Hours 0–2 post-weigh-in: 500–750 mL of ORS or sodium-rich fluid, a small carbohydrate-rich snack.
  • Hours 2–8: Continue steady fluid intake, a moderate meal with carbs and protein, and rest. Sleep if there's time.
  • 2–3 hours pre-fight: top up hydration, eat a familiar and easily digestible pre-competition meal, and stop eating 60–90 minutes before warm-up to avoid GI distress.

A Note on Extreme Cuts

Large cuts — above 7–8% of bodyweight — are associated with real safety risks: cardiac stress, kidney damage, dangerous electrolyte imbalances, and heat stroke. Several combat sports deaths have been directly linked to extreme weight cutting. Beyond the safety issue, the performance evidence is clear: fighters who make extreme cuts lose. The studies consistently show that rehydration time, not the size of the advantage at the higher weight, determines outcomes. A 3% cut with great rehydration beats a 10% cut with poor rehydration every time.

If you're routinely cutting more than 5%, the answer is moving up a weight class or doing serious body composition work in the off-season. Not a bigger sauna suit.

The Practical Takeaway

Build your weight management strategy from the outside in: start with long-term body composition, reduce glycogen stores in the final week, keep the acute water cut under 4%, and execute a structured, sodium-anchored rehydration protocol. The fighters who do this well don't just make weight — they show up to fight night recovered, fuelled, and ready to perform at their actual ceiling.
